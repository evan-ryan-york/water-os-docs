Phase 2-3 Opportunity Analysis

Phase 2: Water Quality Testing & Safe Water Services

Nairobi’s water quality challenges create a strong opening for our SafeWater solutions in Phase 2.

Contamination Severity & Awareness: As detailed, outbreaks of waterborne disease are frequent. Even outside epidemic events, many Nairobi residents express distrust of their water. A 2019 survey in Eastlands estates found 54% were “not confident” in the safety of tap water and 42% routinely treated water at home (boiling or using chlorine) – indicating widespread awareness of contamination risk. This climate means households would value an easy way to verify water quality. Currently, getting water tested involves taking a sample to a lab (like the government chemist or private labs) which is impractical for most. If we introduce affordable household test kits (e.g. strips or digital testers) through kiosks or vendors, uptake could be significant – even if initially just to satisfy curiosity or during scare periods. Also, community health NGOs can incorporate these kits in their hygiene campaigns (for example, giving out test strips during cholera education drives, to motivate behavior change).

Target Customer Segments (Phase 2):

Households in informal settlements: They are most at risk and spend money on boiling (fuel cost) and chlorine tablets. If we sell a low-cost test (say <$1 per test) that instantly shows contamination, families might use it occasionally (e.g. after heavy rains when source water might be dirtier). This is a mass, low-margin market, likely best approached via NGO subsidies or bundling with water purchases (“buy 10 jerrycans, get a free test strip”).

Water Operators (Certification): We can offer vendors a certification service – test their water source periodically and certify them as “Safe Water Provider” if they meet standards. Vendors could display this certification to gain customer trust (a competitive edge). Given some vendors do chlorinate water or source from treated supplies, this could differentiate them. They’d pay a small fee for testing/certification. Nairobi Water’s regulation department might even welcome such a program (it aligns with their goal of safer water in slums without having to police everyone).

Institutions & Utility: Phase 2 also extends to working with Nairobi City Water to monitor quality in the network. The utility performs periodic tests, but resource constraints mean many points (like standpipes in slums) go untested. We could contract with them or with public health authorities to deploy sensor kits or rapid tests at various distribution points, feeding data to a dashboard. This taps into utility budgets (or donor-funded projects for water safety).

Distribution & Partnerships: To reach households, the existing distribution channels will be leveraged. We envision partnering with NGOs active in WASH education – e.g., Kenya Red Cross, which often leads cholera response, could include our test kits in their outreach. They have volunteer networks in each ward. Another channel: pharmacies and kiosks – just as chlorine sachets are sold at shops, our test kits or filters could be sold there. Our Phase 1 operator partners can also act as sales agents; a water truck driver could upsell a test kit to a client (“Would you like to test the water I delivered? Only 50 bob”). Since mobile money is ubiquitous, we might enable ordering test kits via USSD/M-Pesa and deliver through boda bodas (motorbike couriers) – feasible given Nairobi’s e-commerce growth (Jumia etc. deliver small items routinely).

Revenue Potential (Phase 2): While Phase 2 likely won’t be as immediately lucrative as Phase 3, it’s still significant. Nairobi’s ~800k unserved households might realistically spend maybe $1–2 per year on testing or certification if properly marketed – that’s up to $1.6M/year potential. SafeWater’s bigger revenue in Phase 2 might come from value-added services: e.g., selling point-of-use filters or recurring water treatment subscriptions (like delivering chlorine refills) on a B2C basis, or training/certifying 100+ water vendors for a fee. If we certify a vendor and they display a “WaterOS Safe Water” badge, they might pay $10/month for that status (if it brings them more business). By Year 3, a combination of household kit sales, vendor certifications, and maybe institutional contracts (e.g. a donor-funded program paying us to monitor water in 50 schools) could reach $200–400k ARR as projected. Nairobi specifically could contribute a large chunk of that, as it’s an epicenter of these issues.

Phase 2 Strategic Timing: We should likely launch Phase 2 in parallel with late Phase 1 or shortly after (around Month 18). The rationale is twofold: (1) The informal operator network we build in Phase 1 is a direct channel to roll out quality services – we’ll have trust built with vendors and can quickly pilot test kits or certification with them. (2) There is an urgency and political attention on water quality now (due to recent cholera outbreaks prompting even Kenya’s first cholera vaccination campaign

Phase 3: Non-Revenue Water Reduction

Phase 3 focuses on utility partnerships to cut leakage and theft. Nairobi is arguably one of the highest-impact targets in the world for NRW reduction given its 46% losses on a base of ~440 million m³ produced
eastleighvoice.co.ke
.

NRW Reduction Potential: Bringing Nairobi’s NRW from 46% down to, say, 30% would save roughly 70 million cubic meters of water per year (204M → 133M m³ loss). That’s enough to supply ~1.5 million more people at WHO basic needs levels – a huge boon to Nairobi’s water security. Financially, assuming water tariffs around KSh 50 per m³ on average (blended domestic/commercial), that volume is KSh 3.5 billion ($30M) in revenue. Even if the utility only realizes half of that due to needed fixes, the value of NRW reduction is enormous. It perfectly fits a performance-contract model: we propose, for example, “Save 10 million m³ in Year 1 in Zone X, and bill it, then Water OS gets ~30% of the revenue from that saved water.” The utility essentially pays out of newfound revenue, making it politically easier.

Utility Motivation & Alignment: As discussed, Nairobi Water is under intense pressure to reduce NRW – the Auditor-General explicitly flagged illegal connections and leaks, and the Governor will want quick wins before the next election (2027). They’ve tried conventional measures (mass disconnections, replacing some old pipes) but with limited success. A performance-based contract with a tech startup could be appealing if we come through a credible channel. Likely we’d partner with a larger firm or donor for credibility in initial talks (e.g., position Water OS as the technology provider under a World Bank project or a Kenyan PPP unit initiative). Importantly, WASREB (the water regulator) encourages utilities to reduce NRW and has set 20-25% as a target
eastleighvoice.co.ke
eastleighvoice.co.ke
. They might bless our approach if it helps meet sector goals. Also, the Athi Water Works (regional utility service board) might co-sponsor such a performance contract. All in all, Nairobi’s leadership seems open – e.g., the county is already using public-private approaches in other services (waste collection, bus transport), so a PPP for water loss reduction is not far-fetched.

Phase 3 Services & Approach: Our offering would include: IoT monitoring (deploy pressure sensors, smart meters in pilot areas to detect leaks/theft in real time), data analytics (using our software to pinpoint high-loss zones, suspicious usage patterns), and management interventions (working with utility teams to fix issues). We might start with one district metering area (DMA) in Nairobi – perhaps an estate with mixed connections and known high losses – to prove we can cut NRW by, say, 5–10 percentage points via better monitoring and incentivized repairs. Once proven, we scale to more zones. We would need buy-in from utility staff; thus, part of the contract might involve capacity building (training their engineers to use our dashboard) and revenue sharing down to department level (to get field crews invested in success).

Regulatory Framework: Kenya has a PPP Act and a relatively supportive environment for private sector in water on specific tasks. The NWSC (Nairobi Water) is fully government-owned, so any performance contract likely requires county government approval. Given the spotlight on losses, we expect minimal political resistance if structured as “no cure, no pay” – meaning if we don’t deliver savings, they owe us nothing. This aligns with the governor’s interests (they can only win). We should ensure transparency to avoid any perception of backdoor privatization (which is politically sensitive; past attempts to privatize water utilities in Kenya faced protest
thenewhumanitarian.org
thenewhumanitarian.org
). Emphasizing that ownership stays public, and we are just a service provider for efficiency, will be key in stakeholder communications.

Revenue Model (Phase 3): If we save, say, $10M of water revenue in year 1 of a contract, a typical performance fee might be 25–30%, so $2.5–3M to us. Reaching the projected $1–3M ARR in Years 3-5 likely involves 1-2 such contracts. Nairobi could itself yield that if we phased across the city. Alternatively, we might do multiple smaller contracts (e.g., one focusing on commercial metering accuracy, another on leak detection in specific zones). We’ll also explore donor-funded results-based financing: organizations like GIZ or World Bank’s IFC might pay for each m³ of NRW reduced as part of climate resilience funding. Nairobi, being a flagship city, could attract such funding.

Phase 3 Sequencing: Given Nairobi’s scale and urgency, we might actually start groundwork for Phase 3 earlier than originally planned – possibly by Year 2 once Phase 1 has given us local credibility. We could run a small NRW pilot in parallel with Phase 2, maybe with one of the city’s satellite towns (Athi Water manages some neighboring areas that also have high NRW). However, the full utility contract likely will materialize in Years 3–4 after we’ve proven ourselves and lined up partners. Among Phase 2 and 3, Phase 3 has the clearer big-ticket opportunity in Nairobi (multi-million potential), but it also carries longer sales cycles and heavier stakeholder management. Phase 2 (quality) we can execute more unilaterally and quicker, generating goodwill and data that bolster our Phase 3 pitch (“look, we already improved water in slums via SafeWater, now let’s tackle your losses citywide”). Therefore, the plan is: Launch Phase 2 by Year 2 to build health impact narrative, concurrently initiate Phase 3 pilot discussions, aim for a formal NRW contract by Year 3. This sequencing leverages Nairobi’s needs and ensures we are deeply embedded in the city’s water ecosystem by the time large contracts are on the table.

In summary, Phase 2 and 3 in Nairobi represent a one-two punch: Phase 2 addresses the water quality gap left by the utility, empowering consumers and informal providers, while Phase 3 tackles the utility’s efficiency gap to improve the system at its core. Combined, they fulfill our mission of safe, sustainable water – and in Nairobi, both are not only socially impactful but also financially rewarding due to the city’s scale and pain points. The opportunities here align perfectly with Water OS’s long-term vision, reinforcing Nairobi as a top choice to realize our multi-phase strategy.
