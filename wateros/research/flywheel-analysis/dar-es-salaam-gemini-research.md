A multi-region cholera outbreak from 2023-2024, which included Dar es Salaam, resulted in thousands of cases and dozens of deaths, creating heightened public awareness of waterborne disease risks.44 This is not an isolated incident; diarrhea is a leading cause of morbidity, and academic studies have confirmed the presence of fecal coliforms in the utility's tap water at levels deemed unsafe for consumption.45This creates a clear demand for quality verification from households and businesses. The distribution infrastructure to meet this demand is readily available. A national Community Health Worker (CHW) program was recently launched in Dar es Salaam, establishing a trusted, last-mile network perfect for distributing household test kits and promoting hygiene.47 This, combined with the operator network built in Phase 1 and the presence of numerous water-focused NGOs, provides multiple scalable channels to market for SafeWater products and certifications.NRW Reduction Opportunity (Phase 3)The opportunity to partner with the Dar es Salaam Water Supply and Sanitation Authority (DAWASA) on Non-Revenue Water (NRW) reduction is perhaps the most compelling aspect of this market. The utility is facing a "burning platform" moment where the status quo is no longer politically or operationally tenable.Utility Condition and Motivation:DAWASA's NRW is extremely high, with various official reports from the utility regulator (EWURA), government auditors, and the World Bank consistently placing the figure between 37% and 55% in recent years.48 This represents a massive loss of both a precious resource and vital revenue. The situation reached a crisis point in July 2024 when the Minister for Water publicly suspended the Acting CEO and the Director of Production of DAWASA, explicitly citing "unreasonable water loss" as the primary reason.49This public dismissal creates an unprecedented window of opportunity. The utility's leadership is now under intense political pressure to deliver rapid, measurable results on NRW. Internal efforts have proven insufficient, making the leadership uniquely receptive to an innovative, externally-driven solution. A performance-based contract (PBC), where Water OS is paid based on the volume of water saved, directly addresses their need for a low-risk, high-impact intervention. The potential contract value is significant; saving just 10% of lost water could generate millions in annual revenue for the utility and for Water OS.Leadership and Openness:DAWASA's management team is staffed by technically qualified engineers and finance professionals, suggesting they can engage competently on a technical solution.51 While a previous privatization attempt in 2005 failed, the PBC model is fundamentally different, as the utility retains full ownership and control of its assets.52 The current environment of extreme distress likely overrides historical skepticism, creating a strong motivation to partner.Phase 2-3 SequencingBoth opportunities are strong, but the NRW reduction opportunity (Phase 3) is uniquely timely. The political pressure on DAWASA is acute now. Therefore, the recommended strategy is to initiate conversations with DAWASA leadership in parallel with the launch of Phase 1. While Phase 2 (SafeWater) has a clear market need, it can be launched after Phase 1 is established. The urgency and scale of the Phase 3 opportunity with DAWASA make it a primary strategic focus from the moment of market entry.INFRASTRUCTURE & ENABLERS ANALYSISDigital InfrastructureDar es Salaam's digital infrastructure is sufficiently developed to support the operational needs of Water OS.
