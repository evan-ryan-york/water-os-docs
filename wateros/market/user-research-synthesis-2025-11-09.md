# User Research Synthesis
**Version:** 1.0 | **Last Updated:** 2025-11-22

**Research Period:** October 28 - November 7, 2025
**Total Interviews:** 13 participants
**Geographic Focus:** Greater Accra Metropolitan Area (GAMA)
**User Types:** Residential Purchasers (6), Commercial Purchasers (2), Drivers/Fleet Owners (3), Depot Owners (1), Community Resellers (1)

---

## Executive Summary

User research conducted across Greater Accra between October-November 2025 strongly validates Water OS's core business model while revealing critical regulatory and operational challenges that require strategic adjustments. Key findings:

**Validated Assumptions:**
- Trust-based driver relationships dominate residential purchasing (supports 5% commission model)
- Commercial clients experience acute operational pain and are underserved (validates 20% commission opportunity)
- Depot finder addresses massive fuel waste from drivers searching for water (core value prop confirmed)
- Digital payment readiness is high across all user segments
- Pricing assumptions align with market reality (â‚µ300-450 per 1,000L)

**Critical Discoveries:**
- GWCL depot shutdown crisis is more severe than anticipated (systematic, not isolated)
- Drivers traveling 30-50km each way to find water (fuel costs destroying margins)
- Quality concerns are universal but no one is addressing them systematically
- Commercial clients losing customers due to water unavailability (extreme pain point)

**Strategic Implications:**
- Accelerate and deepen commercial B2B focus (earlier start, harder launch requirements)
- Build GWCL regulatory engagement strategy into pre-launch phase
- Position water quality assurance as Day 1 marketing asset, not just compliance
- Consider earlier implementation of fuel cost calculator and depot availability tools

## Part 1: Findings by User Type

### 1.1 Residential Purchasers (n=6)

**Profile:** Households in Kasoa, Nungua, Adenta, Sakumono experiencing unreliable GWCL supply

#### Common Patterns

**Water Access Reality:**
- Tap water flows once per week to once per 3 months, with many areas experiencing complete outages lasting 6-12 months
- Households use mixed strategies: rainwater collection, boreholes, tanker deliveries
- Tanker water is emergency/supplementary source, not primary for most

**Supplier Relationships:**
- **Universal Pattern:** Long-term, trust-based relationships with specific drivers
  - Dorcas: "We've used the same driver for years"
  - Joy: Orders from her neighbor who owns a tanker
  - Enoch: Has two trusted drivers he "always calls"
  - Kwasi Junior: Uses same suppliers for over a year
- Relationships based on familiarity and perceived reliability, not formal verification
- Switching suppliers is rare; happens only when primary supplier unavailable

**Ordering Process:**
- Direct phone calls to known drivers (no intermediaries)
- Payment: Cash on delivery or Mobile Money
- Delivery timing: Quoted 1-4 hours, actual delivery often next day
- No formal scheduling; purely reactive when storage runs low

**Pricing:**
- Range: â‚µ300-600 per delivery (â‚µ350-450 most common for 1,000L)
- Prices fluctuate based on scarcity, distance, fuel costs
- Dry season premium: Prices increase 20-50% during peak demand
- No price transparency; varies by driver and negotiation

**Pain Points (Ranked by Severity):**

1. **Cost Burden (9-10/10):** Water expenses have doubled or tripled compared to GWCL bills. Ama Agata (Sakumono) paying â‚µ300-450 weekly vs. previous monthly bills.

2. **Reliability/Timing (8-9/10):** Drivers quote 3-4 hours, arrive next day. Causes major household disruption.

3. **Quality Uncertainty (7-8/10):** Universal concern but no action. Enoch received "brownish water" once. No one knows actual source. Most assume it's "tap water" without verification.

4. **Scheduling Conflicts (6-7/10):** Deliveries arrive when residents aren't home, requiring rescheduling.

5. **Source Transparency (6-7/10):** Drivers claim "tap water" or "from Tema" but no way to verify.

**Technology Readiness:**
- High smartphone penetration; all use WhatsApp and Mobile Money
- Several have used Uber, food delivery apps
- **Critical Quote - Joy:** "If there was an app where you can just request water, see the price, and pay on delivery â€” like how Bolt works â€” that would really help."
- Trust requirements: Need to see driver ratings/reviews before first use
- Prefer pay-on-delivery over prepayment

**Decision Authority:**
- Typically managed by household head (often male)
- Women in sample (Dorcas, Joy, Ama) either directly manage or heavily influence decisions
- Single-occupant households (Enoch) have full authority

**Key Insight for Water OS:**
Residential purchasers already have drivers. **They don't need us to find them a driver; they need their existing driver to be more efficient and reliable.** The 5% commission model that respects existing relationships while digitizing transactions is exactly right. Attempting to insert new drivers would face massive resistance.
