### Part 2: Why GWCL Shut Down Private Depots

**Key Revelation from GWCL Engineer:**

The engineer explained the real reason depots were shut down - **theft**, not just operational issues.

**How the Theft Works:**
- GWCL sells water to private depots at very low rates (~15 cedis per cubic meter, approximately $1.50)
- Depot owners then sell at higher prices (which GWCL has no problem with)
- However, depot owners were installing **illegal bypass connections** to tap water before the meter
- This means the meter doesn't read the actual amount of water being used
- When caught, depots are fined up to 70,000 cedis (~$7,000)
- Many pay the fine because they were making more money from theft

**The Engineer's Frustration:**
> "Ghana Water is selling it to you at cost. Why then do you still get another connection to steal more water?"

**Current GWCL Strategy:**
- Shut down most private depots
- Opened only 4 public hydrants in Accra that GWCL directly controls:
  1. **37 (GWCL compound)** - meter reads directly, payment goes to GWCL
  2. **Awoshie**
  3. **Tema Free Zone** (recently reopened after being locked)
  4. **East Legon** (Anna to visit next week)

**Scale Problem:**
- 4 hydrants serving ~2,000+ trucks (likely closer to 3,000 now)
- Each truck takes ~30 minutes to fill
- ~10 usable hours per day = 20 trucks per hydrant per day
- 4 hydrants Ã— 20 trucks = **80 trucks/day capacity**
- That's only **~4% of the truck fleet**

> "They're servicing 80 trucks a day out of over 2,000... that's so inefficient."

### Part 3: Private Depot Water Storage Model

**Key Discovery - Reservoir Model:**
Many private depots don't have hydrants. Instead they use:
- Large underground reservoirs built under houses
- Multiple polytanks (one person has 50 polytanks)
- They fill when GWCL turns on water for their area, then sell to truckers

**The Polytank Problem:**
- When GWCL opens taps for a community, large depot owners fill all their storage first
- By the time water reaches residential users, it's gone
- GWCL allocates a fixed volume per community - they don't care who gets it

> "Ghana Water doesn't really care about who gets water or who doesn't. Their issue is that they're releasing water and people are still stealing it."

### Part 4: Borehole Water Demand

**Surprising Finding:**
Some customers specifically request borehole water over treated pipe water because it's cheaper.

**Borehole Limitations:**
- Water is often salty
- Used mainly for non-drinking purposes:
  - Flushing toilets
  - Public washrooms
  - Commercial washing (fish, chicken)
  - Cleaning
- Cannot scale to serve population - would only cover ~0.1% of demand

> "Under optimal conditions, if Accra were able to get as much water as possible from boreholes, it would serve 0.1% of the population."

## Strategic Takeaways

1. **Theft is the Core Problem:** GWCL shut down depots because of bypass connections stealing water, not operational issues. Our meter solution directly addresses this.

2. **4 Public Hydrants Can't Scale:** With only 80 trucks/day capacity vs 2,000+ trucks, GWCL must work with private depots. Our solution enables that.

3. **Simplify the Pitch:** Don't mention GWCL monitoring to depot owners initially. Frame as free data for their own benefit.

4. **Reservoir Depots are Different:** Many private depots don't have hydrants - they store water in polytanks/reservoirs. May need different metering approach.

5. **Start Simple:** Get a meter installed first, worry about digital payments later. Prove the concept before adding complexity.
